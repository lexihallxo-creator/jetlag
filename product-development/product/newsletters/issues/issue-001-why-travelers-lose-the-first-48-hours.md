# Issue 001

## Subject Line

Why smart travelers still lose their first 48 hours after landing

## Opening

Most travelers think jet lag ruins the flight day.

It usually ruins the 48 hours after that.

That is the part that quietly steals the meeting, the dinner, the museum day, the workout, or the first night of real sleep.

## Why This Happens

People focus on the plane and ignore the transition window after arrival.

They either try to power through exhaustion, nap at the wrong time, overuse caffeine, or treat local bedtime like a suggestion. By then, the destination clock and the body clock are fighting in public.

## A Better Framework: The Arrival Window

Think about jet lag recovery as three windows:

1. `Landing window`: protect alertness and avoid chaos decisions
2. `Day-one window`: set the next sleep anchor correctly
3. `Second-day window`: reinforce the new rhythm instead of undoing it

If you miss the first window, the next two get much harder.

## What To Do Instead

- Decide before travel what local bedtime you are aiming for
- Use light intentionally after arrival instead of wandering through the day half-awake
- Be careful with naps because a badly timed nap becomes a vote for the old time zone
- Treat caffeine like a tool with a cutoff, not a panic button

## Scenario

Imagine a traveler flying from New York to Paris for a Monday morning meeting.

If they arrive exhausted Sunday, nap too late, grab caffeine in the evening, and then try to sleep at midnight local time, they often create a second bad night. The real damage shows up Monday afternoon when attention collapses.

A better plan starts before wheels-down. The arrival day is not random recovery time. It is the moment you set the schedule the rest of the trip will inherit.

## Close

The biggest jet lag mistake is treating arrival like downtime when it is actually a setup window.
