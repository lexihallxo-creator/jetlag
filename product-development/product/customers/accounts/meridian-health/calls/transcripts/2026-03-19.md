## HIPAA Audit Trail (7:00 - 22:00)

**Hannah Stulberg:** Dr. Pham, walk us through the audit trail requirements.

**Dr. Sarah Pham:** I need to be able to pull a report that shows every user who accessed a patient-facing prototype, what data it queried, and when. If I can't produce that report during an audit, we can't use Forge for anything that touches PHI. Period.

**Riley Patel:** When you say "what data it queried" — are you referring to the application's database queries, API calls to external systems, or both?

**Dr. Sarah Pham:** Both. Let me give you a concrete scenario. A care coordinator opens a Forge-built scheduling tool and pulls up a patient's appointment history. I need a log entry that records: the coordinator's identity, the timestamp, the action taken — in this case, viewing appointment records — the patient identifier accessed, and the data source queried. That's the minimum for a HIPAA access audit.

**Morgan Wu:** That's significantly more granular than what our current audit system captures. Right now we log authentication events and project-level actions — creating a project, deploying, modifying settings. We don't instrument data access within deployed applications.

**Dr. Sarah Pham:** I understand that's a big engineering lift. But it's non-negotiable for PHI-touching applications. We currently limit Forge to internal tools that don't access patient data specifically because this capability doesn't exist.

**Riley Patel:** Dr. Pham, would it be helpful if we provided an audit logging SDK or middleware that your team could integrate into Forge-deployed applications? Rather than Forge automatically instrumenting every data access — which would require deep integration with your data layer — we could provide the hooks and your engineers could define what gets logged.

**Dr. Sarah Pham:** That's a pragmatic approach. I'd want to see the design. The key requirement is that the logs are tamper-proof and retained for a minimum of six years, which is our HIPAA retention policy.

**Riley Patel:** Six years — noted. That's longer than most of our current log retention. I'll factor that into the proposal.

**Hannah Stulberg:** Riley, let's put together a scoping document and share it with Dr. Pham's team so we can align on requirements before we commit to a timeline.

## Webhook Reliability (22:00 - 34:00)

**Hannah Stulberg:** Anil, you flagged webhook issues in your pre-call notes. Can you walk us through what happened?

**Anil Gupta:** We had three dropped webhooks last month. Each one was a missed notification to a care coordinator. In healthcare, a missed notification isn't an inconvenience — it's a patient safety issue. We need guaranteed delivery with retries.

**Morgan Wu:** Can you tell me more about the integration architecture? What's receiving the webhooks?

**Anil Gupta:** We have a Forge-deployed application that processes lab results and sends a webhook to our EHR middleware — it's a Mirth Connect instance. The webhook triggers a notification workflow that alerts the relevant care coordinator when certain lab values are out of range. In all three failures, the lab results were processed correctly within the Forge app, but the outbound webhook never reached Mirth.

**Morgan Wu:** Did you see any error responses, or did the requests just disappear?

**Anil Gupta:** That's part of the problem — we don't have visibility into webhook delivery status from the Forge side. No delivery logs, no retry attempts, no failure notifications. We only discovered the drops because a nurse noticed that a patient's critical potassium result didn't trigger the expected alert. She caught it during a chart review the next morning.

**Morgan Wu:** That's concerning. Let me investigate what happened on our infrastructure side — there could be timeout issues, network blips, or queuing problems. In terms of the solution, you're right that we need a retry mechanism. Industry standard is exponential backoff with a configurable number of retries, plus a dead letter queue for payloads that exhaust all retries so nothing is silently lost.

**Anil Gupta:** Exactly. And I need a dashboard where my team can see webhook delivery status — successful, pending retry, failed. If something hits the dead letter queue, I want an alert so we can intervene manually.

**Morgan Wu:** That's reasonable. Let me put together a design proposal. Can I also get access to your Mirth Connect webhook endpoint specs? I want to make sure our retry logic handles your specific timeout and response code patterns.

**Anil Gupta:** I'll have my integration engineer send those over this week.

## BAA & Environment Isolation (34:00 - 40:00)

**Dr. Sarah Pham:** Before we move to wrap-up, I need to ask about a Business Associate Agreement. Any vendor that handles or could access PHI needs to sign a BAA. Does Forge have a standard BAA?

**Hannah Stulberg:** We don't have one today, but I understand it's a requirement for healthcare customers. Let me take this back to our legal team and get the process started.

**Dr. Sarah Pham:** Please do. Without a BAA, I can't authorize any expansion into patient-facing applications, regardless of the technical capabilities.

**Anil Gupta:** Related to that — we'd like environment isolation. Right now all of our Forge projects live in the same workspace. I want to be able to designate certain projects as "PHI-capable" with stricter access controls, enhanced audit logging, and separate deployment pipelines. Our internal dashboards and prototyping tools don't need that overhead, but anything touching patient data does.

**Riley Patel:** That's a clean separation model. We've been thinking about workspace tiers or environment labels internally. Your use case is a great design partner scenario — would you be open to collaborating on the requirements?

**Anil Gupta:** Happy to. We've done this with other vendors. I can share the environment isolation spec we use for our cloud infrastructure as a starting point.
