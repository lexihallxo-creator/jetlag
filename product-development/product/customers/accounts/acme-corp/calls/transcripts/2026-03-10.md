## Segment 1: Generation Quality Discussion (3:15 - 14:30)

**Priya Nair:** Sure. The React output is genuinely good. **Priya:** The main gaps are around complex state management. When I describe something that needs shared state across deeply nested components, it tends to prop-drill instead of using context or a state manager. **Jordan:** The Tailwind issue is a known bug we're fixing in the next release. The state management one is interesting. Are you using the architecture hints in your prompts?

**Jordan:** Yeah, let me drop a doc link in the chat. Basically you can add a `.forge/architecture.yaml` file to your project that specifies patterns like "use Zustand for global state" and the model will follow those constraints.

**Priya:** Oh, that would be huge. We have very specific patterns at Acme and if I could just define them once, that would save a lot of post-generation cleanup.

## Segment 2: Deployment Pain Points (14:30 - 28:45)

**Hannah:** Before we go there, Derek, you mentioned deployment is the biggest friction point right now. Can you walk us through what that looks like?

**Derek:** Yeah, so here's the workflow today. Someone on the team builds something in Forge - let's say it takes them 2 hours to go from idea to working prototype. Great. Then they export the code. Now they have to create an AWS infrastructure setup - Terraform files, configure the VPC, set up the load balancer, configure environment variables, set up the CI/CD pipeline. That's another 2 to 3 days of work.

**Tom:** And it's not just the time. It's a completely different skill set. The developers who are great at prompting and prototyping in Forge aren't necessarily the ones who know Terraform and AWS networking. So we end up with a handoff, which introduces delays and context loss.

**Hannah:** How many of your Forge projects end up getting deployed to production?

**Derek:** Right now, maybe 40%. The rest stay as prototypes because the deployment cost is too high relative to the value. If deployment were one click, I think we'd deploy 80% or more.

**Morgan Wu:** What does your ideal deployment experience look like?

**Derek:** I click a "Deploy" button in Forge. It asks me which AWS account, which region, what environment variables I need. Maybe it shows me a preview of the infrastructure it'll create. I confirm, and 5 minutes later it's live with a URL I can share.

**Priya:** And rollback. If something breaks, I want to roll back to the previous version with one click too.

**Morgan:** That's essentially what we're building. Hannah, do you want to share the timeline?

**Hannah:** We're targeting a beta for One-Click Deploy in late April. Derek, would your team be interested in being a beta partner?

**Derek:** One hundred percent. We'd prioritize testing it. That feature alone would justify doubling our seat count.

## Segment 3: Team Collaboration Needs (28:45 - 39:00)

**Hannah:** Let's talk about the collaboration gaps. With 12 engineers now, what's breaking?

**Priya:** The biggest thing is templates. I've built really good project configurations - the architecture.yaml that Jordan just mentioned, custom prompt libraries, component patterns. But there's no way to share them. Every new team member has to set all of this up from scratch, or I have to sit with them and walk through it.

**Derek:** From a management perspective, I have zero visibility into what people are building. There's no feed, no shared workspace, nothing. I found out last week that two engineers were building nearly identical tools because they didn't know about each other's projects.

**Tom:** The version history gap is also a concern. Right now if someone makes changes, the previous version is just gone. We need audit trails, especially as we use Forge for more production workloads.

**Hannah:** All of this resonates with what we're hearing across our customer base. Collaboration is our second biggest workstream after deployment. Let me share what we're thinking.

We're approaching it in three phases. Phase one is shared project templates, where you can publish a project configuration as a template that anyone on your org can use. Phase two is a team workspace with activity feeds, shared project views, and basic permissions. Phase three is the full collaboration suite - comments, review workflows, branching, and merge.

**Priya:** What's the timeline on phase one?

**Hannah:** We're targeting May for shared templates. The team workspace would follow in Q3.

**Derek:** Phase one alone would be a huge quality of life improvement. Would we be able to beta test that too?

**Hannah:** Absolutely. I'll add you to the beta list. Priya, you mentioned you'd be willing to join our customer advisory board for this. We'd love to have you involved in the design process.

**Priya:** Yeah, count me in. I have a lot of opinions about how collaborative development tools should work.
